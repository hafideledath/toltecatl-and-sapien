# Toltecatl and SAPIEN
is a poetry experiment that uses generative AI. Specifically, it's a poetic exchange between two voices, Toltecatl (whose poems are written by me) and SAPIEN (whose poems are written predominantly by a large language model (GPT-4.1) fine-tuned on solely my poetry, and edited by me). 

The point of this project is to see how well AI can write poetry, how consistently it can work with multiple motifs, themes, and extended metaphors across multiple different poems, and how well it can process abstract and esoteric prompts. 

While far from perfect (the AI often falls into repetitive, tired patterns—or, sometimes, it's "too" original, making strange and inconsistent stylistic choices for no apparent reason), the model produced surprisingly good work at times. I found that providing large amounts of context and text from planning documents I had made for myself drastically improved the model's output. 

I've taken steps to ensure there isn't any plagiarised content in the model's outputs, checking every line to make sure it isn't stolen from elsewhere. If I spotted anything, I'd just re-prompt the model or ask it to change the implicated line. 

I understand that even having taken these steps, from an artistic point of view, this project seems ethically dubious. That is partly the point, as is demonstrated through the project's narrative arc—I want to emphasize that AI outputs are a consumable commodity, and art at its best should be hard to digest. I do think generative AI can play a (limited) role in the production of art, and it's precisely in "meta" cases like this, where its usage is disclosed and properly justified. A good existing example of another such case is Sympathy Tower Tokyo by Rie Qudan:

> But the book also grabbed attention because Qudan said that part of it – 5% was the figure given, though she now says that was only an approximation – was written using artificial intelligence. This, she tells me, comprised parts of the novel which are presented as a character’s exchange with ChatGPT. But Qudan also “gained a lot of inspiration” for the novel through “exchanges with AI and from the realisation that it can reflect human thought processes in interesting ways”. Qudan’s use of AI, in other words, seeks not to deceive the reader but to help us to see its effects. 

> One character feels pity for the chatbot, “condemned to an empty life of endlessly spewing out the language it was told to spew, without ever understanding what this cut-and-paste patchwork of other people’s words meant”. [From Author Rie Qudan: Why I used ChatGPT to write my prize-winning novel](https://www.theguardian.com/books/2025/aug/18/author-rie-qudan-why-i-used-chatgpt-to-write-my-prize-winning-novel)

That being said, this is a very narrow exception. I firmly believe in the vast majority of cases, the use of generative AI in human art dilutes the value of said art. I hope you enjoy reading this anthology as much as I enjoyed creating it!
